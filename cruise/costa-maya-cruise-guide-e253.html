Costa Maya, Mexico

Your cruise port guide — from the pier to the jungle ruins and back, with time to spare.

⚠ Port in Transition — Read This First

Royal Caribbean Group took ownership of this port in July 2025 and is redeveloping it into a private resort called Perfect Day Mexico, expected to open in late 2027. Through 2026 and into 2027, you should expect construction activity and possible closures of in-port shops, restaurants, and attractions. The town of Mahahual, all beach clubs, and every excursion destination described in this guide are completely unaffected. If your ship docks here in 2026 or 2027, plan to head outside the port complex — that's where the real day is, anyway.

First Look: The Port

Costa Maya was built for cruise ships — and that's both its limitation and its advantage.

Your ship docks directly at a long T-shaped pier — no tender, no waiting offshore. The pier stretches about 1,115 feet, so you can walk to the terminal or hop the free trolley that loops continuously. When three or four ships are in port simultaneously, the trolley backs up; if you're mobile and it's not peak summer heat, the walk is manageable.

What you'll find at the end of the pier is a purpose-built cruise village: Mayan-motif architecture, a central square where Papantla Flyers perform, a large pool with a swim-up bar, the Aviarius bird walk suspended above the complex, a dolphin encounter facility, and rows of shops and restaurants. It reads more resort than town — because that's exactly what it is. The authentic Mexico is a $4 taxi ride away in Mahahual.

Port Fast Facts

Docking: Direct pier — no tendering. Up to four ships at once. Language: English widely spoken throughout port and Mahahual. Currency: USD accepted almost everywhere; pesos are official. ATMs (both currencies) are in the port. Taxis and small vendors are cash-only — bring small bills. Safety: The port complex and Mahahual are considered safe for independent exploration during the day.

Inside the Port

You don't have to leave — but most of the interesting things happen when you do.

The Main Pool

The centerpiece of the port complex is a large resort-style pool ringed with lounge chairs and backed by a swim-up bar. Chairs are complimentary. It's a genuinely comfortable place to spend a few hours if you've got a late departure or want a no-effort port day — reviewers consistently rate it well. A pair of flamingos can be spotted near the Aviarius entrance at no charge.

Port WalkableCultural Experience

Danza de los Voladores

In the central square, performers known as Papantla Flyers ascend a tall pole and descend in spinning spirals — a traditional ceremony with roots in Veracruz. It runs multiple times throughout the day and costs nothing to watch. Worth pausing for a few minutes even if you're heading straight out to an excursion.

Port Walkable

Aviarius — Bird Aviary

Eight connected habitats suspended on rope bridges 20–30 feet above the port, accessible via a tall wooden staircase. You'll walk through enclosures housing owls, parrots, lovebirds, cardinals, and golden pheasants, with an aerial view of the dolphin pools below. Entry runs approximately $14 for adults and $12 for children (figures confirmed across multiple 2024–2025 reviews — verify on arrival). Not accessible for strollers, wheelchairs, or scooters.

Save the larger seeds in your cup for the Blue Macaws near the exit — they're the best photo opportunity in the whole aviary.

Dolphin Discovery

Located behind the main pool, the dolphin encounter program offers either a poolside experience (approximately $120) or an in-water swim (approximately $140). Figures are from 2023–2024 sources — confirm current pricing at the facility.

Kakaw Chocolate Factory & Tastings

A short chocolate-making experience rooted in Mayan cacao traditions. The duty-free area also typically offers complimentary tequila and mezcal samples. Good for filling a short block of time without leaving the complex.

Mahahual Village

A fishing village that wakes up when the ships arrive — relaxed, walkable, and a different world from the port.

The town of Mahahual sits about five minutes south of the port by taxi. A wide malecón runs from the lighthouse down through town, with the beach on one side and a string of restaurants, beach bars, and small shops on the other. It's compact enough to walk end-to-end in 20 minutes.

On a busy port day — and busy means up to four ships in simultaneously — the malecón fills with cruise passengers and the vendor energy picks up accordingly. If you don't have a beach club booked and you arrive without a plan, you'll spend the first ten minutes declining offers. If you do have something booked, walk directly there. The town is easy to navigate and English is widely spoken.

Getting There

Taxi from port to Mahahual: approximately $4–$5 per person, about 5–7 minutes. Cash only — agree on the fare before you get in. A one-way trolley runs from port to town for approximately $4/person, but local transport regulations require a taxi for the return trip. Golf cart rentals are available at the port exit for approximately $60–$75/day or by the hour. Electric bikes run approximately $20–$25/day.

Snorkeling along the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef — the world's second-largest coral reef system — is accessible directly from the town beach. Multiple Cruise Critic reviewers mention a low-cost snorkel area near the lighthouse; verify current availability on arrival. Beachfront massages are widely reported at approximately $20/hour.

One more thing: the port exit itself can be disorienting. The shopping area is designed to keep you circulating. Look for the transportation area signage toward the back of the complex to find your way to the taxi stand and the street outside.

If your ship stays late, Pizza Papi in Mahahual — wood-fired, funky décor, strong reviews — opens at 5 p.m. It's in the town proper, away from the beachfront crush, which means fewer cruise passengers and a quieter table.

Beach Clubs

The water here is calm and clear — the reef keeps it that way. The question is only which stretch of sand you want to claim.

Almost the entire Costa Maya coastline runs alongside the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, which means calm, shallow water and good snorkeling directly from the beach. The clubs closest to port are a five-minute taxi ride from Mahahual; the more secluded ones sit further south along largely undeveloped coast.

Maya Chan Beach ★

The most consistently recommended beach club in Costa Maya, full stop. It's private and requires advance reservation — on busy port days, it fills completely. A glass-bottom kayak, snorkel gear, floaties, beach BBQ, and unlimited drinks are all included. The location is further south along the undeveloped coast, which means fewer vendors and longer stretches of sand. Expect approximately 15 minutes by taxi from port. Mayan Ruins

Costa Maya sits closer to working archaeological sites than any other major cruise port in Mexico — and most passengers never make it to them.

Before You Go: Logistics

Independent operators are not permitted inside the port facility. Your cruise line's excursion desk is the only option that operates within the complex. Third-party operators — often smaller, better-reviewed, and less expensive — meet passengers just outside the port gates, approximately one to two blocks from the exit. All three ruins sites below are accessed via air-conditioned vans with bilingual guides. Bring cash (USD or MXN) for site entrance fees and the on-site shopping areas. Credit cards are not accepted at the ruins.

Historic SiteNatureCruise ExcursionPrivate Tour Option

Chacchoben Ruins

The first-time choice. About 43 miles from port — roughly one hour on Highway 307 — Chacchoben dates to around 1000 BC with its peak period between 300 and 900 AD. The main draw is the Gran Basamento, a pyramidal platform over 100 feet tall that rises clear of the tree canopy. The site still shows traces of original red paint on the stucco walls, which larger and more-visited sites have lost entirely. Howler monkeys, iguanas, and toucans appear regularly along the paths. The whole excursion runs approximately four hours port-to-port, with about 90 minutes on site.

Walk-in entrance is approximately 65 pesos (around $4 USD) for those going independently, though booking an organized tour is strongly recommended for cruise passengers to ensure a guaranteed return time.

The Chacchoben + Bacalar Lagoon combo is the most popular pairing at this port — ruins in the morning, lagoon by boat in the afternoon. It works best if your ship gives you eight or more hours.

Kohunlich Ruins

For those who want fewer crowds and a more immersive jungle setting. Kohunlich sits about 90 minutes from port and spreads across 21 acres of rainforest, with roughly 200 structures — most still unexcavated beneath the trees. The centerpiece is the Temple of the Masks: enormous carved stone faces, 1,500 years old, staring from the main pyramid walls. Walking here is mostly shaded, and some structures can still be climbed for tree-canopy views. Plan for 1 hour 45 minutes each way. Your ship needs to give you at least eight hours in port, and booking through an operator with a back-to-ship guarantee is essential.

Dzibanché Ruins

Two hours from port and rarely visited. On most days you'll have the main temples to yourself. Founded around 300 BC, Dzibanché is the least-trafficked of the three sites — the Temple of the Captives and the Temple of the Owl can both still be climbed for wide views over unbroken jungle canopy. The drive makes this feasible only on long port days (nine or more hours). Book through your cruise line or an operator with a guaranteed return; this is not a DIY excursion.

📍 Dzibanché Ruins

Bacalar Lagoon

A freshwater lagoon of shifting blues — from pale aqua in the shallows to deep teal in the cenotes, with pirates' history underneath.
NatureCruise ExcursionPrivate Tour Option7+ Hours in Port

Bacalar sits about 80–90 minutes from the port — close enough for a cruise day, far enough that you don't want to wing it. The "Lagoon of Seven Colors" is freshwater, recognized as a Pueblo Mágico by the Mexican government, and genuinely earns the name: shades shift from pale aqua to deep indigo depending on depth and time of day. Tours run on pontoon boats, stopping at the Pirate Channel for swimming and at cenotes for a look at the rare stromatolites — living microbial formations visible just beneath the surface, among the oldest life forms on earth.

Two Things to Know Before You Book

Wednesdays: Boat tours on Bacalar Lagoon pause every Wednesday as part of a conservation initiative. If your port day falls on a Wednesday, boat-free alternatives — beach club visits, cenote experiences — are available instead. Sunscreen: Rinse off all sunscreen and lotions before entering the water. Many products contain chemicals harmful to the stromatolites and the lagoon's ecosystem.

Total excursion time is approximately five hours. Reviewers consistently report returning to port with one to two hours to spare. DIY is not recommended given the drive time and tight cruise schedules — book through a reputable local operator with a guaranteed return time.

What to Know

The logistics, so nothing sneaks up on you.

Money

USD is accepted almost everywhere in the port and Mahahual; prices are often listed in both currencies. ATMs dispensing both pesos and dollars are in the port complex. Taxis, small vendors, and ruins shopping areas are cash-only — bring small bills in USD or MXN. Most established shops in the port take credit cards.

Taxi Tips

Agree on the fare before you get in. Have exact change or small bills. The vast majority of taxis in Quintana Roo do not accept cards. Costs are typically consistent — approximately $4–$5 per person to Mahahual — but some forum posts describe inflated pricing when passengers are rushing back to ship. Leave yourself enough buffer that you're never in that position.

Climate

Hot and humid year-round, with daily temperatures typically running between the mid-70s and low 90s°F (24–33°C). The main cruise season (winter through spring) is drier and less humid. Hurricane season runs June through November. Bring sunscreen, a hat, and insect repellent for jungle excursions. Lightweight clothing and water shoes for beach days.

Crowd Planning

Costa Maya welcomed nearly 600 ship calls in 2024 — and can hold four ships at the pier simultaneously. On heavy days, Mahahual fills fast. Pre-book your beach club (especially Maya Chan, which requires it), and check your port day against the ship schedule before you arrive. The fewer ships in port, the calmer the whole experience.

What to Skip

A few things that sound plausible but will cost you time, money, or your port day entirely.

Lost Mayan Kingdom / Maya Water Park — Closed

This adventure and water park has closed as part of Royal Caribbean's acquisition of the port. It will eventually become part of Perfect Day Mexico's Loco Waterpark. Do not book or plan around it.

Tulum, Cancún, or Playa del Carmen — Too Far

Costa Maya is far south of the Riviera Maya corridor. Tulum alone is roughly a three-hour drive each way. These are not realistic port-day destinations. Any tour pitching them should be declined immediately.

Unbooked Beach Clubs on Busy Days

Showing up without a reservation — especially at Maya Chan — means you may not get in. On days with multiple ships, the best clubs fill by mid-morning. Book before your cruise departs.

DIY Bacalar or Dzibanché

The drive times on both make independent, unguided visits genuinely risky for cruise passengers. The timing math is too tight without a guaranteed return. Use an operator for these.
