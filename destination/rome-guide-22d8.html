Rome

Layered, loud, and utterly unhurried about impressing you.
01 — Orientation & Character

How to Read the City

Rome doesn't organise itself for your convenience — learn its logic and it opens up immediately.

Rome is built on and around seven hills, with the Tiber River curving through the west. The great ancient and Renaissance landmarks — the Colosseum, the Forums, the Pantheon, Piazza Navona — sit on the east bank. The Vatican and Trastevere occupy the west. Think of the city in three daily anchors: Ancient Rome (Colosseum to Capitoline Hill), Vatican Rome (St. Peter's and the Museums), and the Centro Storico (the Pantheon–Navona–Trevi corridor). Most major sights sit within 15–20 minutes of each other on foot. Your best transport is your feet.

One navigational note worth keeping

Street-number logic in the centro is its own system. Odd and even numbers can run on the same side and then switch. Use a map app, not house numbers, when hunting a specific address.

The Neighbourhoods

Centro Storico Do It

Navona · Pantheon · Campo de' Fiori

Baroque fountains, cobbled lanes, espresso bars where Romans have stood for a century. Tourist-heavy, yes, but irreplaceable. A strong first-time base.

↗ Map

Monti Local

Bohemian · vintage · the locals' side of the Colosseum

Rome's oldest rione. Ivy-draped ochre buildings, craft beer bars, vintage boutiques, and the social fountain-square of Piazza della Madonna dei Monti.

↗ Map

Trastevere Do It

Cobblestones · ivy · late nights

Locals still live here — proudly — though the central lanes are tourist-thick by 8 pm. Excellent restaurants and bars. Better for dinner than for sleeping next to.

↗ Map

Testaccio Local

Working-class · market · offal & wine

The old slaughterhouse district, now Rome's most authentic food neighbourhood. Home to the covered Testaccio Market and some of the city's most respected trattorias.

↗ Map

Prati Practical

Wide boulevards · boutiques · Vatican adjacent

Middle-class, genuinely Roman, a short walk to St. Peter's. Via Cola di Rienzo is a serious shopping street. Better hotel value than the Centro Storico.

↗ Map

Aventine & Ostiense Off-Path

Posh quiet · street art · keyhole views

Aventine Hill is a residential enclave with garden terraces and the famous Knights of Malta keyhole. Ostiense below holds Rome's best contemporary street art.

↗ Map

Coppedè Off-Path

Art Nouveau · Neo-Gothic · surreal

A concentrated cluster of fantastical architecture designed 1913–1927. Art Nouveau, Neo-Gothic, and Baroque colliding in one fever dream. Almost nobody goes here.

↗ Map

San Lorenzo Local

Student energy · cheap eats · La Sapienza

Home to Rome's biggest university and the city's most unfiltered nightlife. Trattorias that charge fair prices. Not polished — that's the point.

Esquilino — Your Home Base Your Hotel

Multicultural · well-connected · more Rome than you'd expect

One of Rome's original seven hills, Esquilino sits just south of Termini Station and centres on Piazza Vittorio Emanuele II — one of the city's largest squares, ringed with 19th-century porticoed architecture. It has a reputation that's rougher than its reality: this is a lived-in, genuinely multicultural neighbourhood with layers of history that most visitors never stop to notice.

The neighbourhood's standout landmark is the Basilica di Santa Maria Maggiore, one of Rome's four great papal basilicas, with 5th-century mosaics that rank among the oldest in the city. It's a 10-minute walk from most hotels in the area and almost always less crowded than the Vatican. Pope Francis, breaking with centuries of tradition, chose to be buried here — the basilica has seen a quiet surge in visitors since 2025 as a result. Also worth a detour: the Church of Santa Prassede, a block away, with Byzantine mosaics that stop most people in their tracks.

The Nuovo Mercato Esquilino, tucked behind Piazza Vittorio, is Rome's most genuinely international market — spices, imported produce, and ingredients from dozens of countries alongside Italian staples. Arrive on a weekday morning before 1 pm. The surrounding streets hold some of the best and most affordable ethnic food in Rome: Chinese, Ethiopian, South Asian, and Middle Eastern restaurants operating well outside tourist pricing.

For something unmistakably Roman: Palazzo del Freddo di Giovanni Fassi (Via Principe Eugenio, 65) has been serving gelato since 1880 and moved to its current palace in 1928. Generations of Romans have queued here. Unlike most gelaterias it has actual seating, and the sampietrino — a chocolate recreation of Rome's cobblestones — is the thing to order.

Location advantage: Termini Station puts the entire city on your doorstep. Metro Line A (Termini or Repubblica stops) connects you to the Spanish Steps, Trevi Fountain corridor, and Vatican in under 15 minutes. Metro Line B reaches the Colosseum in two stops. The neighbourhood itself is walkable to Monti in 10 minutes and to the Colosseum in about 25.

Honest note: The blocks immediately around Termini station can feel chaotic and attract petty crime — stay alert at night, keep bags close, and stick to well-lit streets. Two or three blocks away from the station, the neighbourhood settles down considerably.

02 — Top Sights & Experiences

The Non-Negotiables

These are the reason Rome has been on every itinerary for three centuries — and they earn it.

The Colosseum + Roman Forum + Palatine Hill Do It

One ticket, three sites, half a day minimum. The Colosseum is the anchor — genuinely staggering at any hour — but the Roman Forum and Palatine Hill are what give it weight. You'll walk mosaic floors that Augustus himself walked and look out over the Circus Maximus from the hilltop. Allow 3–4 hours for all three together.

↗ Map: Colosseum

Book online weeks ahead — lines reach 500+ people within 30 minutes of opening in peak season. Combined ticket valid two days. Entry around €18 at time of research — verify at coopculture.it.

Vatican Museums + Sistine Chapel + St. Peter's Do It

The Vatican Museums hold one of the largest art collections on earth. Michelangelo's Sistine Chapel ceiling is different in person — the scale and density no photograph conveys. Don't rush past the Raphael Rooms on the way through. St. Peter's Basilica is free to enter; the dome climb costs a modest fee and delivers some of the best views in the city.

↗ Map: Vatican Museums

Book exclusively through museivaticani.va — third-party sellers charge double or sell fakes. Covered knees and shoulders required. St. Peter's interior access was modified during the 2025 Jubilee year; confirm current conditions before you arrive.

The Pantheon Do It

Continuously in use since 125 CE, the Pantheon is the best-preserved ancient building on earth. The unreinforced concrete dome — still the world's largest — and the oculus cut perfectly into its crown remain engineering marvels. Raphael is buried here. Go first thing in the morning before the square fills up; entry now requires a timed slot booked online.

↗ Map: The Pantheon

Experience it as a pause. Stand under the oculus and look up for a full minute. The tourists who rush through miss what they came for.

Galleria Borghese Do It

A villa in Villa Borghese park holding Bernini sculptures and paintings by Caravaggio, Raphael, and Titian in the rooms where Cardinal Scipione Borghese originally placed them. Entry is strictly limited — two-hour timed visits only, which creates a genuinely intimate encounter with works that would be mobbed anywhere else.

↗ Map: Galleria Borghese

Book as far in advance as possible — slots go fast. The surrounding gardens are free and a good midday retreat from the heat.

Trevi Fountain Do It

Nicola Salvi's Baroque fountain is the largest in Rome, and watching Oceanus preside over horses surging out of the palazzo wall never fully loses its charge — even through the crowds. Free to visit. One coin ensures your return, two brings romance, three leads to marriage. Toss right-handed over your left shoulder.

↗ Map: Trevi Fountain

Arrive before 7:30 am or after 10 pm — midday is a press of bodies. Especially atmospheric at night when the floodlights take over.

Piazza Navona Do It

Built over an ancient Roman stadium — the elongated oval shape still visible from above. Three fountains anchor the square; the centrepiece is Bernini's Fountain of the Four Rivers, representing four continents. Street performers, outdoor cafés, golden-hour light. Free to visit; best in the evening as the daytime density thins.

↗ Map: Piazza Navona

Castel Sant'Angelo Do It

Emperor Hadrian's mausoleum, later a papal fortress and refuge. The rooftop terrace delivers solid views over the Tiber and the city. Quieter than the Vatican and well worth combining on the same day. Plan about an hour.

↗ Map: Castel Sant'Angelo

Capitoline Museums Do It

The world's oldest public museums. The original Marcus Aurelius bronze, the Dying Gaul, and direct terrace views down to the Roman Forum. Plan two hours. Consistently overlooked, which makes it one of the better crowd-light experiences in the city centre.

↗ Map: Capitoline Museums
03 — Food & Drink

What to Eat, and Where

Roman food is not complicated. It is precise. Those are not the same thing.

The Roman canon — order all four before you leave

Cacio e pepe: Pecorino Romano, black pepper, pasta water — simpler and harder than it looks. Carbonara: egg yolk, Pecorino, guanciale — no cream, ever. Amatriciana: guanciale, tomato, Pecorino. Gricia: guanciale and Pecorino, no tomato — the foundation the others were built on.

Beyond pasta: supplì (fried rice balls, €2–3), carciofi alla giudia (whole fried artichokes), porchetta (herb-roasted pork, best at markets), pizza al taglio (sold by weight, eaten standing).

Dining rhythm — commit to it

Breakfast 7–10 am · Lunch 12:30–3 pm · Aperitivo 6–9 pm · Dinner 7:30 pm (locals eat 8:30 pm+). Don't arrive expecting quick service — arrive expecting to linger. Menus with photos near monuments: keep walking. Book popular spots 2–3 weeks ahead.

Felice a Testaccio Local

Open since 1936. A waiter finishes mixing your cacio e pepe directly at your table until every tonnarello is coated. Retro checkered floors, unmistakably local atmosphere. Book well in advance. Via Mastro Giorgio, 29, Testaccio.

↗ Map

Research prices: cacio e pepe around €14–22. Verify current hours and reservations before you go.

Roma Sparita Local

The late Anthony Bourdain championed this Trastevere trattoria for its cacio e pepe, which arrives in a crispy, golden bowl made entirely of Parmigiano-Reggiano. One of the more theatrical presentations in a city not known for theatrics. Piazza di Santa Cecilia, outdoor seating year-round.

↗ Map

Da Enzo al 29 Local

One of Trastevere's most consistent trattorias — silky, peppery cacio e pepe, strong carbonara, no-nonsense Roman cooking. Via dei Vascellari, 29. Book ahead; it doesn't take walk-ins easily.

↗ Map

Roscioli Local

Both a serious deli counter and a proper restaurant. Aged Pecorino for extra depth in the cacio e pepe, tonnarelli for the right texture. Also excellent for cheese, charcuterie, and wine. Via dei Giubbonari, near Campo de' Fiori. Reservations essential.

↗ Map

Testaccio Market Do It

Rome's finest covered market: fresh pasta, artisan cheeses, supplì, seasonal produce, and street food vendors. Lunch here — €8–15 — beats most restaurant meals in the tourist centre. Open Monday–Saturday mornings.

↗ Map

Pizzarium Bonci Off-Path

Gabriele Bonci's pizza laboratory in Prati. Light, airy Roman-style dough sold by weight with seasonal toppings. Unlike anything in the tourist centre. Via della Meloria, 43. Research price: €6–10.

↗ Map

Jewish Ghetto — Carciofi alla Giudia Do It

Rome's Jewish community has been here for over 2,000 years — the oldest in Europe. Whole fried artichokes, baccalà, and particular cacio e pepe variations. Nonna Betta and Ba'Ghetto are both well-regarded. Research price: €20–35 per person.

↗ Map

Coffee — how Romans do it

Stand at the bar; it costs less than sitting. Order a caffè (espresso), drink it in two sips, leave. A cappuccino after noon marks you as a tourist immediately. In summer, granita di caffè is essential. Sciascia Caffè 1919 in Prati is a historic institution.

Bar breakfast (espresso + cornetto): roughly €2–4. Sitting at a terrace near the Pantheon: expect to pay notably more.

Gelato — what's real

Artisanal gelato sits in covered metal pans — not mounded in neon piles. Gelateria Torcè (Viale dell'Arte, EUR district) has won Gambero Rosso awards. Avoid any gelateria within sight of a monument that doesn't use covered pans.

04 — Outdoor & Nature

Parks, Hills, Ancient Roads

Rome is greener than its reputation — the parks and hilltops offer something the monuments cannot.

Villa Borghese Do It

Rome's answer to Central Park: a sprawling hilltop garden with umbrella pines, a small rowing lake (~€3 for 20 min), bike rentals (~€12/hr), and the Galleria Borghese at its heart. Gardens are free. Good midday retreat from the heat. The Pincio terrace delivers city views worth the walk up.

↗ Map

Via Appia Antica — The Appian Way Off-Path

Ancient Rome's most important road, flanked by umbrella pines, tomb ruins, and the Catacombs of San Callixtus and San Sebastiano. On Sundays the road closes to traffic — this is the day to go, on foot or rented bike. Haunting, quiet, and genuinely removed from the tourist circuits.

↗ Map

The paving stones are original — gorgeous but uneven. Bring water and shoes with grip.

Giardino degli Aranci + Aventine Hill Off-Path

A small terraced garden on Aventine Hill with a scenic terrace overlooking the Tiber and St. Peter's dome. Free, uncrowded, at its best in late afternoon. Pair with the Aventine Keyhole nearby and the adjacent Rose Garden — particularly in April–May bloom.

↗ Map

Villa Doria Pamphilj Local

Rome's largest public park — 184 hectares of gardens, fountains, and the 17th-century Casino del Bel Respiro. Frequented by Romans running, picnicking, and walking dogs. Almost no tourists. Open sunrise to sunset, free.

↗ Map

Janiculum Hill (Gianicolo) at Sunset Off-Path

The best panoramic view of Rome. Not one of the official seven hills — it's actually higher than most of them — and the Gianicolo offers a complete sweep of domes and spires. Locals know this is where you watch the sunset. A cannon fires every day at noon; don't be startled if you're nearby.

↗ Map

Parco degli Acquedotti Off-Path

Ancient Roman aqueducts marching across a suburban meadow — one of the most unexpectedly cinematic landscapes in the city. Popular with locals for sunset walks and picnics; almost never on tourist itineraries. Southeast Rome, reachable by metro (Subaugusta). Free.

↗ Map
05 — Shopping & Markets

Where Romans Actually Buy Things

The best Rome shopping happens where the tour groups don't.

Porta Portese Flea Market Local

Sundays only, 6 am until around 2 pm. Trastevere's southern edge transforms into Rome's largest flea market: antiques, vintage clothing, vinyl records, and genuine rubbish to sort through. A Sunday ritual for Romans since 1945. Come early for the best finds.

↗ Map

Watch your wallet — markets concentrate pickpockets. Keep bags zipped and in front of you.

Campo de' Fiori Market Do It

Rome's oldest market, in this location since 1869. Monday–Saturday mornings: fresh produce, spices, olive oils, gourmet goods, and flowers. The square becomes a bar and restaurant hub by evening. Primarily a food market — not a souvenir hunt.

↗ Map

Monti — Independent Boutiques & Vintage Local

Rome's best neighbourhood for independent shopping: vintage clothing, handcrafted jewellery, local designers, artisanal leather goods. Concentrated on Via del Boschetto and Via Leonina. The real alternative to the tourist-oriented boutiques near the Spanish Steps.

↗ Map

Via Cola di Rienzo — Prati Practical

Prati's broad shopping avenue: major Italian brands, accessible boutiques, and gourmet food shops — Castroni is essential for fine Italian provisions. Frequented by locals, not tour groups.

↗ Map

Via Condotti & the Tridente — Luxury Practical

Gucci, Prada, Valentino, Bulgari — the luxury flagship mile runs from the Spanish Steps down Via Condotti. Even if it's beyond your budget, the window displays and 19th-century architecture justify a walk.

↗ Map

Via Margutta — Art Galleries & Antiques Off-Path

A picturesque lane near Piazza di Spagna lined with small galleries, artists' studios, and antique shops. Audrey Hepburn's character lived here in Roman Holiday. Quieter and more atmospheric than the surrounding luxury district.

↗ Map
06 — What Most Visitors Miss

The Discoveries

Rome rewards anyone willing to look through a keyhole, descend a staircase, or step into an unmarked courtyard.

The Aventine Keyhole Off-Path

At Piazza dei Cavalieri di Malta on Aventine Hill: a green gate belonging to the Priory of the Knights of Malta. Look through the keyhole and a perfectly framed view of St. Peter's dome appears through a tunnel of manicured garden trees. Free. There's usually a short line. Twenty seconds of pure, inexplicable delight.

↗ Map

Galleria Doria Pamphilj Off-Path

A private art collection inside a grandiose palace on Via del Corso. Velázquez's portrait of Pope Innocent X, Caravaggio's Rest on the Flight into Egypt, Bernini sculptures, and a mirrored gallery where the rooms feel infinite. Intimate in a way the Vatican Museums cannot be.

↗ Map

Basilica di San Clemente — The Lasagna Church Off-Path

Near the Colosseum, this church descends through three distinct layers: a 12th-century basilica above, a 4th-century basilica below, and at the bottom a 1st-century Roman house and a functioning Mithraic temple. You can hear an underground stream rushing through the lowest level. Consistently undervisited.

↗ Map

Palazzo Spada — Borromini's Optical Illusion Off-Path

Baroque architect Francesco Borromini used forced perspective to make a 9-metre corridor appear nearly four times longer. Easy to pair with Campo de' Fiori nearby.

↗ Map

San Luigi dei Francesi — Free Caravaggio Off-Path

A church near Piazza Navona with three Caravaggio paintings in the Contarelli Chapel — the St. Matthew cycle, completed 1599–1600. Free to enter. One of the most significant works in European art, accessible to anyone who walks through the door. Bring coins for the light-box timer.

↗ Map

Galleria Sciarra — Art Nouveau Courtyard Off-Path

Off Via Marco Minghetti, steps from the Trevi Fountain: an Art Nouveau courtyard with ironwork, stained glass, and painted frescoes. Now a private office building, but the courtyard is open to the public. Keep your voice low and look up. Almost nobody knows this is here.

↗ Map

Free First Sunday museums

On the first Sunday of every month, all state-run museums and archaeological sites offer free entry — including the Colosseum and Palatine Hill. Crowds are significant; arrive early. Check the official MIBAC list for current participating locations.

The nasoni — Rome's free drinking fountains

Rome has over 2,500 nasoni — small cast-iron street fountains with clean municipal water. Fill your bottle; skip paying for plastic. When you find one with two holes, cover one — the other shoots a jet upward for easy drinking. Saves €3–5 a day.

07 — Practical Information

Timing, Money, Movement

Rome rewards the prepared traveller and is less forgiving of the careless one.

April–May and September–October are the sweet spots: 18–25°C, manageable crowds, everything open. Summer means 28–35°C and maximum density, though the longest days compensate. August sees Romans evacuate — local restaurants close for 2–4 weeks. Winter offers 30–40% lower hotel prices and shorter lines; November and January are genuinely underrated.

How Many Days

3–4 days covers the main sights without rushing. Five days is where Rome stops feeling like a checklist. Seven days lets you actually live in it.

Day 1: Ancient Rome — Colosseum, Forum, Palatine Hill, Capitoline Museums
Day 2: Vatican — Museums, Sistine Chapel, St. Peter's, Castel Sant'Angelo
Day 3: Centro Storico — Pantheon, Navona, Campo de' Fiori, Trastevere evening
Day 4+: Galleria Borghese, off-path discoveries, Appian Way, Testaccio food circuit

Getting There

FCO Fiumicino — 35–45 min from centre.

  • Leonardo Express: 32-min train to Termini. Best value.
  • Taxi: Fixed fare ~€55 (official white taxis only).
  • By train: Florence ~1.5 hr · Naples ~1 hr · Venice ~3.5 hr
Getting Around

Walking — most centro sights 15–20 min apart.

  • Metro A & B: Vatican → Ottaviano; Steps → Spagna. Single ~€1.50. 7-day ~€24.
  • Bus: Extensive but slow. Avoid Bus 64.
  • Taxis: Official white taxis or FreeNow only.
  • Car: Do not drive in the city centre.
Budget Benchmarks
  • Budget: ~€80–120/day
  • Mid-range: ~€170–255/day
  • Comfort: €300+/day

Hidden costs: Tourist tax €3–7/night · Coperto €2–4 · Audio guides €5–8. Book 3–4 months ahead for spring/autumn.

Cash & Cards

Carry €50–100 cash. Taxis frequently refuse cards. Markets and small trattorias often cash-only.

Use bank ATMs only — skimming devices documented at standalone tourist-zone machines. Always shield your PIN.

08 — What to Avoid

Traps, Scams, and Honest Caveats

Rome is generous to those who pay attention — these are the situations where people stop paying attention.

Restaurants adjacent to major monuments Avoid

Any restaurant within eyeline of the Colosseum, Trevi Fountain, Pantheon, or Vatican is charging a monument premium for average food. If someone is standing outside calling you in and waving a menu, keep walking. Two streets back, prices drop and quality improves.

Bus 64 — Termini to Vatican Avoid

Locally nicknamed the "wallet express," this route is Rome's most notorious pickpocket corridor. Metro Line A (Termini → Ottaviano) is safer, faster, and only marginally less convenient.

Unlicensed taxis and airport touts Avoid

Unlicensed drivers prey on jet-lagged arrivals at Fiumicino. Only use official white taxis with the "TAXI" logo and an active meter. Fixed legal fare from FCO to central Rome is currently around €55 — confirm before getting in.

Third-party Vatican ticket sellers Avoid

Sellers outside the Vatican charge double or sell fake vouchers not honoured at the door. Book exclusively through museivaticani.va. No exceptions.

The "free gift" — roses, bracelets, petitions Avoid

Someone approaches and offers a flower, a woven bracelet, or asks you to sign a petition. The moment you accept or engage, aggressive demands for cash follow. Decline firmly and keep moving.

Ticket machine "helpers" Avoid

Scammers loiter near metro ticket machines and offer unsolicited help. While they assist you, a companion pickpockets you. Ask a uniformed staff member at the nearby booth if you need help.

August in the tourist core Avoid

Many local trattorias close for 2–4 weeks in August. Tourist-area restaurants stay open and exploit the gap. Orient yourself toward Testaccio, Prati, or San Lorenzo if you visit in August.

Arriving at peak-season sights without tickets Avoid

At the Colosseum or Vatican without pre-booked tickets in April–October: plan on a 90-minute to 2-hour line regardless of when you arrive. Security adds 15–25 minutes on top. The three non-negotiables: Colosseum, Vatican Museums, Galleria Borghese.
