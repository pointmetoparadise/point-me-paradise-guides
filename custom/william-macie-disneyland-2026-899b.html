The shape of your trip

Three nights, two park days, five people with very different ride appetites — here's how it all fits together.

You've got a lovely setup: a family-friendly hotel a short walk from the gates, two days of park tickets, and a crew that spans a four-year-old to two adults. The single most important thing to understand up front is the rhythm of your days, because two pieces don't line up automatically — and planning around the gap is what makes the week feel easy instead of rushed.

The one thing to plan around You have three hotel nights but only two days of park tickets. That's a feature, not a problem — it means you get a built-in rest day. Most families burn out trying to do Disney on back-to-back full days with little kids in summer heat. Your middle day is the secret weapon: sleep in, hit the hotel waterpark, nap, and go back into a park refreshed in the evening. More on exactly how to sequence this in "Your two days" below.

Your tickets are 2-day, one-park-per-day — so each park day you pick either Disneyland Park or Disney California Adventure and stay there (no hopping between the two on the same day). With two days, the natural move is one full day in each park. I'll lay out which park suits your crew best on which day further down.

A quick orientation to the two parks, since they're right across a plaza from each other:

  • Disneyland Park — the original. Castle, Fantasyland dark rides, the classic "Disney" feeling. This is the one your little ones will light up in — the highest concentration of gentle, no-height-requirement rides anywhere.
  • Disney California Adventure (DCA) — the second park, just across the entrance plaza. More thrill rides, Pixar Pier, Cars Land, Avengers Campus. A bit more skewed toward bigger kids and adults, but with real gems for the littles too.

Both are walkable from your hotel, and both are covered in this guide. Let's start with home base.

Your home base

Courtyard by Marriott Anaheim Theme Park Entrance — close enough to walk back for a nap, with a waterpark the kids won't stop talking about.

You chose well for a family your size. The Courtyard Theme Park Entrance is a Disneyland "Good Neighbor" hotel built around exactly your situation: room to sleep five, a short walk to the gates, and an on-site waterpark that becomes its own attraction.

Do you get early park entry? — the honest answer No — and it's worth being clear so no one's disappointed at the gate. Good Neighbor hotels like the Courtyard do not include early park entry or extra "magic hours." And here's the part that trips up returning visitors: as of January 5, 2026, Disney ended early entry even for its own three on-site hotels, replacing it with a one-time free Lightning Lane perk for those Disney-hotel guests. That perk doesn't extend to Good Neighbor hotels either. So everyone — you included — enters at the same official open time. The good news: your edge isn't early entry anyway, it's that you're a short walk away. You can be first in line at rope drop without a long drive or tram, which is its own real advantage (covered in the rope-drop and hours sections below).

The walk to the parks

The hotel sits on South Harbor Boulevard, and it's roughly an 8–10 minute walk to the main entrance plaza between the two parks. That's genuinely close — in some cases a shorter walk than from the Disney-owned hotels. The real value of being this close: you can walk back midday for the four-year-old's nap or a swim, then return refreshed. With little kids, that midday reset is worth more than any single ride.

Surfside Waterpark

The hotel's 20,000-square-foot waterpark — slides, a splash zone, and a large hot tub, open year-round — is a real draw and a big part of why this hotel works for your rest day. Reservations are recommended, so check at the front desk when you arrive. Knowing this is here is what makes the no-park middle day a treat rather than a wasted day.

Your room

The family rooms here are known for sleeping larger groups comfortably — many feature bunk beds (a hit with kids), dinette seating, and large bathrooms with dual sinks and a separate standalone shower in addition to the tub. Some rooms face Disney and can catch the fireworks from the window or balcony — worth asking about at check-in, though it depends on availability. The Bistro on-site serves Starbucks coffee and breakfast in the morning and dinner with cocktails in the evening.

Know before you arrive A few honest logistics so nothing catches you off guard: parking is valet only (no self-park), running roughly $40–47 per night plus tax — but because you're walking to the parks, you skip Disney's separate parking fee entirely. There's no free breakfast and no shuttle (you won't need one — it's a walk). Hotel check-in is 4:00 PM and check-out is 11:00 AM; if you arrive before your room is ready on July 20, they'll typically hold your bags so you can head to a park or the pool.

If you'd ever rather drive and park at the parks themselves — say for an easier load-out on a tired evening — Disney's official parking page has current rates, structure locations, and tram details. For your stay, though, the walk from the hotel will almost always be faster and cheaper.

Lightning Lane, explained simply

Disney's paid skip-the-line system. It's optional — but on a summer day with three kids, it can hand you back hours.

Disneyland no longer has the old free FastPass. The current system is called Lightning Lane, and it's a paid, optional way to wait in a much shorter line for popular rides. Here's the plain-English version of the two types you'll actually use.

Lightning Lane Multi Pass — the one most families buy

A per-person, per-day purchase (pricing is dynamic and varies by date — it has started around $34 per person per day, and can run higher on busy summer days). It lets you reserve return times for a set of participating rides — book up to 3 in advance, and once you've used one in the park, you can keep adding more, one at a time, as the day goes on. When you buy it, your PhotoPass photos for that day are included too.

Children under 3 don't need their own pass to ride with your group — so for ride purposes, Brooks (4) does need one, but a child still in arms wouldn't. All five of you would each need a Multi Pass on a day you buy it.

Lightning Lane Single Pass — pay-per-ride for the headliners

A separate, one-ride purchase for a couple of the highest-demand attractions that aren't in the Multi Pass — most notably Star Wars: Rise of the Resistance at Disneyland and Radiator Springs Racers at DCA. You buy these individually, and prices vary by day. You don't have to buy these at all — but if your crew wants a specific headliner without a long standby wait, this is how.

Your booking window — important Because you're staying at a Good Neighbor hotel (not a Disney-owned one), you can purchase Lightning Lane and make your advance selections 3 days before your first park day, starting at 7:00 AM Pacific. Disney-hotel guests get 7 days; you get 3. Set an alarm for 6:55 AM Pacific that morning — the best return times for the popular rides go quickly. (Disney-hotel guests also get a free one-time Lightning Lane perk on check-in; that one does not apply at the Courtyard, so plan to purchase.)

Is it worth it for your family?

Honestly — on a summer day with a 4-, 6-, and 9-year-old, usually yes, at least for your busier park day. The math isn't really about money saved; it's about meltdowns avoided. Standing in a 60-minute standby line in July heat with little kids is where good days go sideways. That said, you don't have to buy it for both days. A reasonable play: buy Multi Pass for your bigger, busier park day, and rope-drop your way through the calmer day without it. I'll help you decide once we see which dates you're locked into and the crowd forecast.

The golden rule of using it well

The mistake families make is "wasting" a Lightning Lane on a ride that's a walk-on anyway. Use Lightning Lane for the rides with genuinely long lines, and use rope drop (next section) and standby for everything else. Book your next Lightning Lane the moment you tap into your current one — while you're still walking the queue. That keeps a steady drip of short-wait rides going all day.

The free strategy that beats everything

Rope drop: the first hour is golden

Being at the gate when the park opens is the single most powerful — and free — time-saver there is.

"Rope drop" means arriving before the park opens and being among the first in when they let guests into the lands. For roughly the first hour, wait times are a fraction of what they'll be by midday. One family rides a dozen attractions without ever feeling rushed; another arrives at 11 and waits 90 minutes for a single ride. The difference is almost entirely this.

Your morning, step by step Aim to be walking out of the Courtyard about 45–60 minutes before official park opening. Walk to the entrance plaza, have your tickets ready in the app, go through security and the turnstiles, and position yourself toward the front. When the rope drops, the first hour is yours. Yes, this means an early start on vacation — but one early morning buys you more rides than any amount of money. With kids, do this on at least your first park day.

A note for your crew specifically

With little ones, you don't need to sprint to the most intense ride. The goal is to knock out the popular kid rides that build long lines — things like Peter Pan's Flight (one of the worst lines in the park all day), the Fantasyland dark rides, and the gentle headliners — while the park is quiet. Hit those first, then settle into a slower pace as the day warms up and lines grow. Save the lower-wait rides and Toontown play time for the busy afternoon hours.

There's no need to overthink which land to run to first. A simple, effective opening for a family in Disneyland Park: head straight to Peter Pan's Flight (its line never gets short), then sweep the nearby Fantasyland dark rides — Snow White's Enchanted Wish, Mr. Toad, Pinocchio, Alice in Wonderland — before the crowds arrive. You'll have ridden five or six things before many guests have finished their coffee.

Rider Switch: how the grown-ups still ride

The single best tool for parents of a little one — it lets both adults ride the big stuff without waiting twice.

Here's the problem Rider Switch solves: Brooks (4) is too short for Space Mountain, but both you and your partner want to ride. Without Rider Switch, one of you waits the whole line, rides, comes back, and then the other waits the entire line all over again. Rider Switch means you only wait once.

How it works

  • Tell the cast member at the ride entrance you want to use Rider Switch.
  • Adult A waits in line (or uses your Lightning Lane / Single Rider) and rides, while Adult B waits outside with Brooks.
  • When Adult A returns, Adult B can go ride without waiting in the full line again — often taking one of the older kids along who wants to re-ride.
  • This pairs beautifully with Lightning Lane: use your Lightning Lane return time for the first adult, and the Rider Switch pass covers the second.
The bonus most people miss Because Henley (9) and possibly Ately can ride the bigger attractions, Rider Switch often lets one of the older kids ride twice — once with each parent. So your 9-year-old becomes the lucky one who rides Big Thunder back-to-back while the four-year-old waits with a parent each time. Everyone wins, and no one stands in a long line more than once.

Sequencing your three days

Two park days and one rest day — here's the rhythm that keeps a little-kid summer trip from ending in tears.

You have flexibility in which days you use your two tickets across your July 20–23 stay. The structure I'd suggest for a family with young kids in summer heat puts a breather in the middle. Adjust the park order once we confirm crowd forecasts, but the shape holds:

Day 1

Disneyland Park — your big day

Start here while everyone's fresh. This is the park your littles will love most, and the one worth rope-dropping. Be at the gate ~45 min before opening, sweep Fantasyland's dark rides (Peter Pan first), then Toontown and the gentle classics. Consider Multi Pass for this day. Walk back midday for Brooks's nap if needed, then return for the evening — fireworks if your night has them.

Day 2

Rest & the waterpark — no park ticket

Your secret weapon. Sleep in, let everyone recover, spend the day at Surfside Waterpark, nap, and explore Downtown Disney in the evening (no ticket needed — shops, dining, and the atmosphere are all free to wander). This is the day that makes the other two great. Skipping it is the most common way families overdo Disney with little ones.

Day 3

Disney California Adventure — your second day

Refreshed and back at it. DCA leans a little bigger — Cars Land, Pixar Pier, Toy Story Mania for everyone, Radiator Springs Racers for the brave. Use Rider Switch and Single Rider so the adults and Henley get the thrills while Brooks does the gentler Pixar Pier rides. A calmer, slightly more grown-up day to close out the trip.

One scheduling reality Your hotel and park dates give you room to arrange the two ticket days as works best — but the exact days you can enter each park depend on your specific tickets and any park-reservation requirements. I'll confirm the bookable days with you so we lock Disneyland and DCA onto the right dates, with the rest day in between. Don't finalize the order until we've checked that together.

A realistic daily flow with little kids

The pattern that works: rope drop hard, rest midday, return refreshed. Mornings are cool and uncrowded — bank your big rides then. Early afternoon in July is hot and busy and exactly when a four-year-old melts down, so that's your window to walk back to the hotel for a nap and a swim. Come back around dinner for the evening's lower crowds, the lit-up castle, parades, and fireworks. Two shorter, well-timed visits beat one death-march day every time.

Eating well without the wait

Mobile order is the move, character meals are the memory, and bringing your own snacks saves real money.

Mobile order — use it every time

The biggest food tip in the parks: order quick-service meals through the Disneyland app's mobile order. You pick a restaurant and an arrival window, order and pay in the app, tap "I'm here" when you're close, and walk up to grab your food — skipping the long order line entirely. With three kids, not standing in a counter line during the lunch rush is a genuine sanity-saver. Book your window an hour or two ahead, especially at peak meal times.

Character dining — worth booking for your kids

For a family with young children, a character meal is often the most-remembered part of the trip — the characters come to your table while you eat, so there's no separate line to wait in. These book up well in advance, so reserve as early as you can (in the app or through Disney dining). Here's the full current lineup at the Disneyland Resort:

Inside the parks

  • Minnie & Friends – Breakfast in the Park · Plaza Inn, Disneyland Park — an all-you-care-to-enjoy breakfast buffet where you meet Minnie and other Disney pals. A lovely way to start a Disneyland morning, and it's right inside the park. (Requires valid park admission for that day.)

At the resort hotels (no park ticket needed)

  • Goofy's Kitchen · Disneyland Hotel — the lively, beloved classic. Chef Goofy and a rotating cast of characters work the room during fun, busy breakfast and dinner buffets. The most kid-energetic of the bunch.
  • Storytellers Cafe · Disney's Grand Californian Hotel — a bountiful buffet themed around California folklore, with character visits. A calmer, well-regarded setting that's a little less frenetic than Goofy's.
  • Disney Princess Breakfast Adventures · Napa Rose, Grand Californian — the premium, special-occasion option: a three-course breakfast with activity time and personal visits from Disney Princesses. If Ately (6) loves the princesses, this is the showstopper — it's pricier and books up fastest, so grab it early if it appeals.
How to choose for your crew With a 4-, 6-, and 9-year-old, any of these lands well. For a single, easy pick that fits your park days, Minnie & Friends at Plaza Inn is the most convenient — book it before rope drop on your Disneyland day and you start the morning fed and photographed. If you want one "wow" meal and Ately is princess-obsessed, Princess Breakfast Adventures is the memory-maker (reserve the moment booking opens — it sells out). Goofy's Kitchen is the all-ages crowd-pleaser if you'd rather a relaxed dinner off-park on your rest day.

Special dining experiences — especially the show packages

Beyond the character meals, Disney offers a handful of bookable "dining events." A few are tailor-made for your family because they bundle a meal with reserved viewing for the big nighttime shows — which solves the hardest part of seeing them with little kids: not having to stake out a curb spot 45 minutes early with three restless children. The ones worth knowing for your trip:

  • Fantasmic! Dining Package (Disneyland Park) — a meal at a participating restaurant that comes with a reserved viewing area for Fantasmic! Given how crowded that show gets, this is the easy way to see it without the long stake-out.
  • Plaza Inn Dining Package (Disneyland Park) — a meal at Plaza Inn paired with reserved seating for a show or the parade. A nice pairing with your Disneyland evening, and Plaza Inn is family-friendly.
  • World of Color Dining Package (DCA) — a meal at a participating restaurant plus a reserved spot for World of Color. The simplest way to lock in a good view on your DCA day.
  • World of Color Dessert Party (DCA) — a seated dessert-and-drinks experience with a premium viewing location for the show. A sweeter, slightly more special-occasion version of the above.
  • Tomorrowland Skyline Terrace (Disneyland Park) — a reservable dining event; a calmer, elevated spot to eat in the park.

One more, for the grown-ups: Trader Sam's Mixology Seminar at the Disneyland Hotel is an adults-only cocktail seminar — a fun date-night idea if you can trade off kid duty one evening, or one to file away for a future trip.

Worth it for you? For a family seeing Fantasmic! or World of Color for the first time, the show dining packages are often worth it purely for the sanity — a reserved view without herding tired kids to a spot early and holding it. They book up, so if you want one, reserve it when you lock your character meal. Bring your own — it's allowed Disneyland lets you bring in outside food and non-alcoholic drinks. Pack the backpack with water bottles, snacks, and a few sandwiches, and you'll save a meaningful amount over three days while keeping the kids fed on their schedule, not the park's. Refill water bottles at fountains, and grab one shared iconic treat (a churro, a Dole Whip) as the splurge rather than buying full counter meals for five every time. And one money-saver that adds up fast in July heat: you can ask for a free cup of ice water at any quick-service restaurant — no purchase required. (This works at the counter-service spots with seating and fountain drinks, not the snack carts.) Bottled water runs around $4+ each, so for a family of five, free cups and refilled bottles save real money across the trip. Some quick-service spots even have self-serve drink stations where you can fill your own.

A couple of treats worth seeking out

Two genuinely good, slightly-hidden snack spots: the Tropical Hideaway behind the Tiki Room (Dole Whip and bao buns in a lovely tucked-away setting), and the classic hand-dipped corn dogs from the Little Red Wagon on Main Street — a long-running fan favorite. Neither requires a reservation.

Little extras & quiet details

The small, mostly-free things that make your family feel like they're in on a secret.

Mail a real postcard from Main Street

There are working vintage mailboxes on Main Street, U.S.A. Drop in a postcard and it actually gets mailed — a sweet, cheap souvenir the kids can send to themselves or grandparents.

Hunt for Hidden Mickeys

Subtle three-circle Mickey shapes are tucked all over the parks — in ironwork, ride scenes, even the landscaping. Make it a family game; it turns waiting in any line into a treasure hunt and costs nothing.

Spot Walt's apartment above the firehouse

Look at the window above the Main Street Fire Station — a lamp is always left lit. It marks Walt Disney's private apartment, where he stayed while overseeing the park. A small piece of history hiding in plain sight.

Find the fortune-teller machines

Esmeralda on Main Street and Fortune Red in New Orleans Square dispense fortune cards — a small, delightful (and cheap) moment kids love. A handful of quarters goes a long way.

Ask about signing the secret guest book

At some attractions, the very first guest to ride that day can sign a hidden guest book. It's elusive and rarely happens, but if you've rope-dropped and you're among the very first on a ride, ask a cast member — it's a real bit of Disney magic.

Celebrate something — get a button

Stop by Guest Relations for a free celebration button (birthday, first visit, etc.). Cast members notice them and often add a little extra magic — an early-trip stop that pays off all week.

The pet cemetery at the Haunted Mansion

Tucked beside the Haunted Mansion queue is a tiny pet cemetery with funny, punny headstones. A fun, not-too-spooky detail to point out to the older kids while you wait.

Press a souvenir coin — the $1 keepsake kids love

Look for the pressed-coin ("penny press") machines all over the resort — there are 150+ collectible Disney designs, including 70th Anniversary ones, starting at $1 each. Heads up on a recent change: most machines now take cards or bills and supply the coin for you, so the old "bring your own pennies and quarters" routine mostly isn't needed anymore — but they're still a cheap, fun souvenir each kid can pick and crank for themselves. A little collection grows fast over a trip.

The most important tip of all Slow down. With little kids, the temptation is to chase ride count, but the moments your family will actually remember are the unhurried ones — Brooks meeting a character, the kids splashing in Toontown, a churro on a bench watching the castle. Build in margin. A day with eight rides and three unhurried pauses beats a day with fourteen rides and three meltdowns.
Before you walk over

What to put in the park bag

Pack once, well, and you'll handle a long summer day with three kids without a single emergency store run.

Portable charger(s) + cable

Non-negotiable. Your phone runs the tickets, app, and photos all day.

Refillable water bottles for everyone

July in Anaheim is hot. Refill at fountains; hydration prevents most kid meltdowns.

Sunscreen + hats

Reapply at the midday break. Little ones burn fast in the open queues.

Snacks (allowed in!)

Pack the bag with your own — saves money and keeps the kids fed on their schedule.

A light stroller for Brooks

Even a four-year-old who "doesn't need one" will, by mile three. It doubles as a snack/jacket cart and a nap spot. Check current stroller size rules in the app.

A change of clothes for the youngest

Spills, splashes, accidents. A spare outfit in a zip bag saves an afternoon.

A small first-aid kit + any meds

Band-aids for little feet, plus any medications. There are first-aid stations in both parks if you need more.

A poncho or two

For water rides and the rare summer shower — cheaper packed than bought in the park.

A photo trick for peace of mind Take a quick phone photo of each kid in the morning in what they're wearing that day. If anyone wanders off in a crowd, you've got an instant, accurate description to show a cast member. Also teach the kids to find any cast member (name tag) if they're lost — Disney's team is exceptional at reuniting families.
Tie something bright to the stroller Strollers get parked in big rows outside attractions, and cast members often move them to tidy the area — so you'll come off a ride to a sea of near-identical strollers, frequently not where you left it. Tie a bright ribbon, bandana, or balloon to the handle (or clip on something colorful) so you can spot yours instantly from a distance. A small piece of duct tape with your name inside the canopy helps too, and a cheap rain cover keeps the seat dry if it's moved during a surprise sprinkle.
